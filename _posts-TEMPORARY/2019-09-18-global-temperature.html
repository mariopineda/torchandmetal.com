Ever since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution, when humans started burning fossil fuels for energy, the world has been getting warmer. This warming has accelerated in recent decades as we continue increasing our use of fossil fuels. Eighteen of the 19 warmest years all have occurred since 2001, with 2016 ranking as the warmest on record. The global temperature record measures the average temperature over the entire surface of the planet. The temperatures we experience locally and in short periods can fluctuate significantly due to predictable cyclical events (night and day, summer and winter). But the global temperature mainly depends on how much energy the planet receives from the Sun and how much it radiates back into space — quantities that change very little. The amount of energy radiated by the Earth depends on the chemical composition of the atmosphere, particularly the amount of heat-trapping greenhouse gases.

A one-degree global change is significant because it takes a vast amount of heat to warm all the oceans, atmosphere, and land by that much. In the past, a one- to two-degree drop was all it took to plunge the Earth into the Little Ice Age. A five-degree drop was enough to bury a large part of North America under a towering mass of ice 20,000 years ago.
